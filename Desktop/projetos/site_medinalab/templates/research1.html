Adoptive Cell Therapies and Epigenetic Modulation
This research line focuses on enhancing adoptive cell therapies (ACTs), such as chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T) and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), through the modulation of epigenetic mechanisms. These advanced therapeutic strategies utilize the patient’s own immune cells, which are expanded or genetically engineered ex vivo and reinfused to target and eliminate cancer cells.

Our work aims to investigate how epigenetic regulation—heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the DNA sequence—can be leveraged to improve the functionality, persistence, and antitumor efficacy of adoptive therapies. By modulating transcriptional pro grams, we seek to overcome tumor-induced immunosuppression and cellular exhaustion, which are major barriers to ACT success in solid tumors.

This research involves both molecular profiling and functional assays to identify key epigenetic targets that enhance the therapeutic potential of immune cells. Ultimately, our goal is to integrate epigenetic modulation into the design of next-generation adoptive immunotherapies, contributing to more durable and effective treatment options for cancer patients.
Tertiary Lymphoid Structures and the Tumor Immune Landscape
This research line investigates the spatial organization of immune cells within the tumor microenvironment, with a particular focus on tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS). These ectopic lymphoid aggregates resemble secondary lymphoid organs and are associated with improved immune responses in several cancer types.

We aim to understand how immune cells coordinate locally to mount effective antitumor responses and how the presence, composition, and maturation of TLS correlate with patient outcomes, particularly in the context of immunotherapy. By characterizing these structures in solid tumors, we seek to identify immunological and molecular signatures that can serve as predictive biomarkers for treatment response.

Our studies combine virtual microscopy, immunohistochemistry, virtual microscopy, and spatial analysis to map immune infiltration and TLS formation in patient samples.
